\begin{abstract}
\vi{Ensuring fairness among users competing for shared network resources remains a critical challenge in multi-client adaptive video streaming. While modern adaptation strategies optimize individual Quality of Experience (QoE), they often overlook the complex trade-offs between different fairness dimensions and playback stability. This study presents an analytical characterization of these trade-offs by evaluating six diverse adaptation strategies—ranging from heuristic-based baselines to fairness-aware reinforcement learning—under identical, highly variable network conditions. We employ a multi-metric framework that jointly analyzes four complementary fairness indicators: Time-Fair QoE (TF-QoE), QoE Fairness Score (QFS), Bitrate Fairness Index (BFI), and Stability-Aware Fairness (SAF). By analyzing experimental data from a 10-client edge-assisted testbed replaying real-world 4G/3G traces, we reveal structural conflicts between quality equity and adaptation instability. Our findings demonstrate that gains in bitrate fairness often come at the expense of increased quality switches for specific users, a trade-off that is significantly amplified under constrained network conditions. This work provides a rigorous observational baseline for the design of equitable streaming systems, highlighting that effective fairness evaluation must account for the interplay between resource distribution and temporal stability.}
\end{abstract}



% The increasing demand for adaptive video streaming presents significant challenges for content providers, particularly in ensuring high Quality of Experience (QoE) and fair bandwidth distribution among users with varying network conditions. This work proposes a Reinforcement Learning (RL)-based approach that leverages Edge Computing capabilities to optimize QoE and fairness in adaptive streaming jointly. Our framework is deployed in a Docker-based infrastructure and tested using real-world network traces to simulate various network congestion levels. The experiments compare heuristic-based ABR methods with our RL-based approach, evaluating key QoE metrics such as average throughput, buffering time, and quality variations. Additionally, we assess fairness using Jain’s Fairness Index to measure how well the system distributes quality among users with different bandwidth constraints. Our results demonstrate that the RL-based approach significantly enhances fairness while improving overall QoE, particularly in highly variable and degraded network conditions ensuring smoother quality transitions and reduced playback disruptions. These findings highlight the potential of RL for advancing adaptive streaming strategies in Edge Computing environments, addressing key limitations of traditional ABR methods.

\section{Introduction} \label{sec: Introduction}
    \vi{The evolution of edge-assisted video delivery has transformed Adaptive Bitrate (ABR) streaming into a complex multi-agent resource contention problem. In this paradigm, the edge layer serves not only as a cache but as a critical coordination point to manage client-side conflicts. While modern adaptation logic effectively optimizes individual Quality of Experience (QoE), the structural interactions between concurrent clients sharing constrained resources remain insufficiently characterized. Traditional fairness evaluation relies heavily on aggregate indices like Jain's Fairness Index (JFI), which typically ignore the temporal and stability-related disparities that define modern edge service experience. 

    This study deconstructs these disparities through a multi-metric observational analysis across four dimensions: temporal quality distribution (TF-QoE), distributional fairness (QFS), bitrate allocation (BFI), and adaptation stability (SAF). By evaluating six distinct ABR strategies under real-world 4G/3G network traces, we expose the "fairness-stability trade-off"—a structural conflict where gains in resource equity often drive increased playback instability for a subset of the client fleet. Our findings indicate that network variability acts as an amplifier for these conflicts, revealing that systems appearing "fair" under stable conditions frequently exhibit severe temporal unfairness during transient congestion windows at the edge node.

    The main contributions of this work are: (i) an analytical characterization of fairness-stability trade-offs explicitly tailored for edge-assisted environments; (ii) the identification of the "tactical panic" and "recovery lag" phenomena as drivers of temporal unfairness in decentralized ABR; and (iii) a set of design recommendations that demonstrate how findings from observational studies can inform the development of superior fairness-aware controllers, such as those employing edge-coordinated Reinforcement Learning (RL).

Section \ref{sec:tradeoff_methodology} describes the evaluation methodology. Section \ref{sec:results_discussion} presents the analytical results. Finally, Section \ref{sec:Conclusion and Future Work} concludes the paper.}

    \vi{Adaptive Bitrate (ABR) streaming allows clients to dynamically select video representations based on network conditions and playback states. DASH-based implementations (e.g., DASH.js) use heuristic rules—typically throughput-based or buffer-based—to balance quality, rebuffering, and stability~\cite{jiang2012improving,yin2015control,spiteri2020bola}. While effective for individual users, these strategies lead to resource contention and unfair quality allocation in multi-client environments. Data-driven approaches like reinforcement learning (RL) \cite{mao2017neural} have improved individual QoE but often treat fairness as a secondary, post-hoc concern. Standard indicators like Jain’s Fairness Index (JFI) \cite{jain1984quantitative} further obscure temporal dynamics and recovery delays. Since fairness is inherently multi-dimensional, recent studies emphasize the need for complementary metrics that capture distributional equity and playback stability~\cite{hossfeld2017definition,hossfeld2018new,akhtar2020fairness}, motivating the joint evaluation of fairness and stability trade-offs in edge-assisted systems.}

    \subsection{Overview of the Analytical Approach}
    
    The objective of the proposed methodology is to analyze trade-offs between multiple fairness metrics and Quality of Experience (QoE) indicators in multi-client adaptive streaming systems. Rather than optimizing a specific objective, the approach focuses on interpreting how different adaptation strategies influence fairness dimensions under shared network conditions. All analyses are performed offline using execution logs from controlled experimental scenarios, ensuring that adaptation decisions remain unaffected by the evaluation process.
    
    A structured analytical workflow is adopted in which executions are grouped into observation sets, complementary fairness metrics are computed independently, inter-metric correlations are evaluated, and adaptation strategies are mapped into a fairness--stability interaction space. This enables systematic identification of metric conflicts and trade-off regions under identical network conditions.

    
    \subsection{Observation Sets and Experimental Grouping}
    
        To ensure consistency and interpretability, experimental results are organized into observation sets that group executions sharing comparable network characteristics. Each observation set corresponds to a specific class of network behavior, such as relatively stable conditions or highly variable throughput patterns. By avoiding the aggregation of heterogeneous scenarios, the analysis prevents misleading conclusions caused by uncontrolled variability. Within each observation set, all ABR strategies are evaluated using the same set of clients, network traces, and execution parameters. This grouping enables direct comparison of fairness outcomes and QoE stability across adaptation strategies, highlighting how identical conditions can lead to different fairness profiles.
    
    \subsection{Multi-Metric Fairness Characterization}
        \vi{To move beyond single-index evaluation, we define four fairness indicators that quantify distinct aspects of resource distribution and playback stability:

        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{TF-QoE (Time-Fair QoE):} Measures the relative deviation of client $i$'s composite QoE score ($Q_i$) from the session average ($\bar{Q}$):
            \begin{equation}
                TF\text{-}QoE_i = 1 - \frac{|Q_i - \bar{Q}|}{\bar{Q} + \epsilon}
                \label{eq:tf_qoe}
            \end{equation}
            where $\epsilon$ is a small constant ($10^{-6}$) for numerical stability.

            \item \textbf{QFS (QoE Fairness Score):} An application of Jain's Fairness Index (JFI) over the temporal average of composite QoE score samples ($\hat{Q}_i$):
            \begin{equation}
                QFS = \frac{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \hat{Q}_i\right)^2}{N \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{N} \hat{Q}_i^2}
                \label{eq:qfs}
            \end{equation}

            \item \textbf{BFI (Bitrate Fairness Index):} Evaluates fairness in quality level allocation using JFI on average numeric representation indices ($\bar{L}_i$):
            \begin{equation}
                BFI = \frac{\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \bar{L}_i\right)^2}{N \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{N} \bar{L}_i^2}
                \label{eq:bfi}
            \end{equation}
            where $\bar{L}_i$ ranges from 1 (360p) to 5 (2160p).

            \item \textbf{SAF (Stability-Aware Fairness):} Characterizes the equity of adaptation stability by considering the count of quality switches ($S_i$):
            \begin{equation}
                SAF = 1 - \frac{|S_i - \bar{S}|}{\bar{S} + \epsilon}
                \label{eq:saf}
            \end{equation}
        \end{itemize}}

    \subsection{Fairness Metric Behavior Across Adaptation Strategies}

    \subsection{Implications for Fairness-Aware ABR Design}
        \vi{Our results suggest that achieving holistic fairness requires moving beyond individual bitrate maximization. We propose three actionable design shifts:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Inter-Client Stability Constraints}: ABR controllers should incorporate the variance of switch counts $\sigma(S)$ into their objective functions. Designers should penalize strategies where specific users suffer disproportionate quality oscillations compared to the fleet average (SAF-aware control).
            \item \textbf{Synchronized Recovery Windows}: To mitigate the TF-QoE "recovery lag," edge-assisted controllers should utilize cross-client synchronization to coordinate quality upswitches, preventing a subset of clients from monopolizing throughput during bandwidth recovery.
            \item \textbf{Asymmetric Reward Penalties}: In cellular-dominant environments, fairness-aware RL reward functions ($R$) should prioritize the standard deviation of perceived QoE over the session average ($\bar{Q}$), explicitly treating fairness as a stability-linked temporal dimension.
        \end{itemize}}

    \subsection{Discussion and Implications}
    
        \vi{From an analytical perspective, the results show that fairness trade-offs are structural properties emerging from the interaction between adaptation logic and network variability, rather than anomalies of specific algorithms. Fairness in multi-client adaptive streaming is inherently multi-dimensional, and no single ABR strategy consistently dominates across all fairness metrics and QoE stability indicators. Instead, strategies occupy distinct regions of the fairness–stability space, reflecting implicit trade-offs between responsiveness and conservatism. Improvements in distributional fairness, bitrate allocation, or temporal QoE exposure do not necessarily occur simultaneously. Aggressive adaptation may enhance certain fairness metrics while degrading playback stability, whereas conservative strategies preserve stability at the cost of increased long-term disparities. Network variability amplifies these effects: under stable conditions, fairness outcomes tend to converge, while highly variable environments expose latent conflicts between fairness dimensions. These findings highlight the limitations of single-metric evaluation and carry direct implications for fairness-aware ABR design. Single-metric optimization is structurally insufficient in multi-client environments, and adaptation mechanisms—particularly reinforcement learning policies—should incorporate multi-dimensional evaluation criteria that explicitly account for network variability as a fairness amplifier.}



        \vi{A critical aspect of our evaluation is the balance between scale and architectural fidelity. While simulators (e.g., ns-3 or Python-based ABR simulators) can handle hundreds of agents, they often abstract away the system-level overheads of real video players. Our choice of 10 concurrent clients—each running a full Chromium instance via Selenium and a high-fidelity DASH.js player—ensures that our findings capture real-world resource contention (CPU/Memory cycles) that is absent in simulation. These implementation-heavy dynamics are essential for characterizing the "tactical panic" phenomena described in Section~\ref{sec:results_discussion}. For larger scales (50+ users), our analytical framework suggests that edge-assisted coordination becomes mandatory to mitigate the resource-heavy penalties of decentralized adaptation, providing a clear roadmap for future 5G-Advanced and 6G streaming optimization~\cite{alsader2025qoe}.}

\section{Conclusion and Future Work \label{sec:Conclusion and Future Work}}
    \vi{This study demonstrates that fairness trade-offs are structural properties of multi-client adaptive streaming, rather than anomalies of specific algorithms. Through multi-metric analysis, we show that fairness is inherently multi-dimensional and cannot be captured by single indicators. Different ABR strategies occupy distinct regions of the fairness--stability space, reflecting trade-offs between responsiveness and conservatism. Our results reveal that network variability acts as a fairness amplifier, exposing latent conflicts obscured under stable conditions. Rather than a universally optimal strategy, this work emphasizes the need for multi-dimensional evaluation frameworks that account for interacting metrics and network dynamics. Future work will investigate how these insights can inform the design of edge-assisted, multi-objective learning strategies to balance competing fairness dimensions.}
